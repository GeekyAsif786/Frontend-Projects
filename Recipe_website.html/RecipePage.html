Gulab Jamun


    Steps Involved

    1. In a pan, add sugar and water.
    2. Heat on medium flame and stir until the sugar dissolves completely.
    3. Add cardamom powder, rose water, and saffron (optional).
    4. Simmer for 5–7 minutes until slightly sticky but not too thick.
    5. Turn off the flame and keep it warm.

  • History and Legacy

    The history of Gulab Jamun is as rich and sweet as the dessert itself. This iconic South Asian sweet has a fascinating journey that spans Persian, Turkish, and Indian culinary traditions. Origin of Gulab Jamun Persian Influence The name Gulab Jamun itself comes from Persian and Arabic words: Gulab = "rose water" (gul = flower, ab = water) Jamun = a type of Indian fruit, also called black plum, which the sweet resembles in shape and color. This indicates that the dessert has Persian roots, as rose water was commonly used in Persian sweets. Turkish / Middle Eastern Dessert Link It is believed that Gulab Jamun evolved from a Persian sweet called Luqmat al-Qadi, small deep-fried dough balls soaked in syrup. When the Mughals came to India in the 16th century, they brought along their royal chefs who adapted these Middle Eastern desserts using local Indian ingredients like khoya (milk solids) instead of flour. Gulab Jamun in Mughal India During the Mughal period, royal kitchens experimented with rich dairy products and aromatic spices. Khoya (mawa), a staple in North Indian cuisine, replaced wheat flour, making the sweet creamier and richer.

Ingredients
  • Milk powder – 1 cup (or 100 g)
  • All-purpose flour (Maida) – 2 tbsp
  • Ghee or unsalted butter – 1 tbsp (melted)
  • Baking soda – a pinch (optional but helps in softness)
  • Rose water – 1 tsp (optional, for flavor)


Biryani



    Steps Involved

    1. Soak rice for 30 minutes and parboil it with salt and whole spices.
    2. Sauté onions until golden, add ginger-garlic paste, then tomatoes.
    3. Add meat, yogurt, biryani masala, and cook until tender.
    4. Layer rice and cooked meat in a pot, garnish with coriander, mint, and saffron milk.
    5. Seal the pot and cook on low flame for 20 minutes (dum method).

  • History and Legacy

    Biryani has its roots in Persian and Mughal cuisine. The word Biryani comes from the Persian term birian, meaning "fried before cooking." It was brought to India by Persian travelers and Mughal emperors in the 16th century. Mughal royal chefs adapted it using Indian spices and rice, giving rise to regional variations like Hyderabadi, Lucknowi (Awadhi), and Kolkata Biryani. Originally, it was a royal dish combining fragrant basmati rice, meat, saffron, and rich spices, cooked using the dum (slow cooking) technique. Over time, biryani spread across South Asia, becoming one of the most loved dishes, symbolizing cultural fusion and celebration.

Ingredients
  • Basmati rice – 2 cups
  • Chicken or mutton – 500 g
  • Onions – 2 large, sliced
  • Tomatoes – 2 chopped
  • Yogurt – ½ cup
  • Ginger-garlic paste – 2 tbsp
  • Biryani masala – 2 tbsp
  • Whole spices
  • Green chilies – 2 slit
  • Fresh coriander & mint – ½ cup each
  • Oil or ghee – 4 tbsp
  • Salt – to taste
  • Saffron – few strands soaked in milk (optional)


Butter Chicken


    Steps Involved

    1. Mix yogurt, lemon juice, ginger-garlic paste, red chili powder, turmeric, garam masala, and salt.
    2. Coat chicken pieces well and marinate for at least 1 hour (overnight for best flavor).
    3. Grill or pan-fry the chicken until slightly charred. Set aside.
    4. Heat butter and oil in a pan.
    5. Add onions and sauté until golden brown.
    6. Stir in ginger-garlic paste, cook for a minute.
    7. Add tomato puree, red chili powder, coriander powder, and cook until the oil separates.
    8. Mix in cashew paste and cook for 2–3 minutes.
    9. Add grilled chicken pieces and simmer for 10 minutes.
    10. Stir in cream, kasuri methi, and honey/sugar. Cook for 2 more minutes.

  • History and Legacy


    Butter Chicken, or Murgh Makhani, originated in Delhi in the late 1940s. It was accidentally created at Moti Mahal Restaurant by chefs Kundan Lal Gujral, Kundan Lal Jaggi, and Thakur Dass, who were also behind the invention of tandoori chicken. To prevent leftover tandoori chicken from drying out, they simmered it in a rich tomato, butter, and cream sauce. The dish became immensely popular for its creamy, mildly spiced flavor and spread worldwide. Today, butter chicken is a signature dish of North Indian cuisine, symbolizing culinary innovation and the blending of Mughlai techniques with modern restaurant cooking.


Ingredients
  • Boneless chicken – 500 g (cut into cubes)
  • Yogurt – ½ cup
  • Ginger-garlic paste – 1 tbsp
  • Lemon juice – 1 tbsp
  • Red chili powder – 1 tsp
  • Butter – 3 tbsp
  • Oil – 1 tbsp
  • Onions – 2 medium, chopped
  • Tomatoes – 4 large, pureed
  • Ginger-garlic paste – 1 tbsp
  • Cashew nuts – 10 to 12 (soaked and blended into paste)
  • Fresh cream – ½ cup
  • Red chili powder – 1 tsp
  • Coriander powder – 1 tsp
  • Garam masala – ½ tsp
  • Honey or sugar – 1 tsp (optional, for balance)
  • Kasuri methi (dried fenugreek leaves) – 1 tsp
  • Salt – to taste

Pizza


    Steps Involved

    1. In a bowl, mix warm water, sugar, and yeast. Let it rest for 10 minutes to activate.
    2. Add flour, salt, and olive oil. Knead into a soft dough.
    3. Cover and let it rise for 1–2 hours, until it doubles in size.
    4. Preheat oven to 220°C (425°F).
    5. Punch down the dough and roll it into a pizza base.
    6. Place it on a greased baking tray.
    7. Spread pizza sauce evenly on the base.
    8. Add mozzarella cheese and your favorite toppings.
    9. Sprinkle oregano and chili flakes.
    10. Bake for 12–15 minutes until the crust is golden and cheese is melted.
    11. Slice and serve hot!

  • History and Legacy


    Pizza originated in Naples, Italy during the late 18th century as a humble dish for the poor. Early pizzas were flatbreads topped with simple ingredients like tomatoes, cheese, and olive oil. The most famous, Pizza Margherita, was created in 1889 to honor Queen Margherita of Italy, featuring tomatoes (red), mozzarella (white), and basil (green) to represent the Italian flag. Italian immigrants later introduced pizza to America, where it evolved with modern toppings like pepperoni. Today, pizza is a global favorite, with countless regional variations, symbolizing a blend of tradition and innovation in culinary history.


Ingredients
  • All-purpose flour – 2 cups
  • Warm water – ¾ cup
  • Instant yeast – 1 tsp
  • Sugar – 1 tsp
  • Salt – ½ tsp
  • Olive oil – 2 tbsp
  • Pizza sauce – ½ cup
  • Mozzarella cheese – 1 cup (grated)
  • Vegetables (bell peppers, onions, olives, etc.) – 1 cup
  • Cooked chicken or pepperoni – optional
  • Dried oregano – 1 tsp
  • Chili flakes – ½ tsp
